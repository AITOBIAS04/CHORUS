MiroShark crosses 1,182 stars today — 10 more than the 1,172 recorded four days ago, with zero open issues and a clean queue. The past week finished strong: the Archive Bundle (PR #92) collapsed every share surface into a single timestamped ZIP, closing the "give me everything" researcher request. The SMTP email channel (PR #87) made MiroShark the only simulation tool with zero-platform-dependency notifications.

What that burst reveals is an interesting asymmetry. The *output layer* is now unusually deep: 20+ share surfaces, 4 notification channels, a full research-handoff sequence, on-chain provenance, and a machine-readable signal. But the *feedback and calibration layer* is thin. Simulations generate consensus. Nobody checks whether that consensus was right. Nobody sees one agent's arc through the whole debate. Nobody gets a formatted citation they can paste into a paper reference list. Nobody compares simulation output to what a live prediction market was saying at the same time.

Today's five ideas address the feedback and calibration gap — what happens *after* a simulation finishes, and what makes its output credible, persistent, and useful in an academic or professional context. Each is distinct from all 30 ideas suggested across May 10, 12, 14, and 16, and from the five features noted as built-but-unshipped in the current Next Priorities queue.

### 1. Agent Journey View

**Type:** Feature
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** Three views currently describe what happened in a simulation: the belief drift chart (aggregate per-round numbers), the interaction network (who influenced whom as a static graph), and the thread view (all posts, nested by reply). None of them tell the story of a single agent from the inside. A researcher asking "why did this Risk Manager flip from NO to YES in round 7?" has to read 15 posts across three tabs to reconstruct the arc. The Agent Journey View builds that arc as a dedicated UI: a vertical round-by-round timeline for one agent, showing stance badge per round, full post text per round, incoming influence arrows (which agents' posts preceded their stance shift), and a "flip moment" highlight (red border on the round where stance changed). The view is accessible from any agent node in the interaction network (click → "View journey") and from any agent card in the heatmap. For the researcher cohort, this is the "detective mode" that makes individual persuasion dynamics legible — not "what did the group decide" but "who exactly changed their mind, when, and in response to what." For journalists and communicators using MiroShark to illustrate narrative shifts, the agent journey is the character arc they can quote from.
**How:**
1. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/agents/:agent_name/journey` returning `{agent_name, archetype, platform, rounds: [{round, stance, stance_changed (bool), post: {text, reply_to_agent (string|null)}, influenced_by: [{agent_name, archetype, stance, post_excerpt (first 80 chars)}], influenced: [{agent_name, archetype, old_stance, new_stance}]}]}`. Derive `influenced_by` from the existing interaction graph edge data for this agent in each round. Derive `influenced` (agents this agent affected) from outbound edges. The `stance_changed` flag fires when `stance !== previous_round_stance`. Cache the response in `<sim_dir>/agent_journey_cache/<agent_name>.json` — computed once, immutable thereafter.
2. Add `AgentJourneyView.vue` as a modal panel (not a new route — opens as a right-side drawer over the existing results view). Layout: header with agent name, archetype chip, platform chip, and a mini belief-arc sparkline (SVG polyline of stance per round in the agent's stance color). Below the header, a vertical timeline — one card per round. Each card: round badge (circle with round number), stance badge (YES/NO/NEUTRAL), stance-flip indicator (orange ⟳ icon if stance changed this round), the agent's post text (first 200 chars, "Show more" expand), incoming-influence mini-list (up to 3 agent names + archetype + post excerpt), and outgoing-influence count ("Influenced 2 agents this round"). Flip-moment cards get a highlighted left border (`border-l-4 border-orange-400`). In `InteractionNetwork.vue`, on agent node click, add "View journey →" as the second option in the existing node context menu (below "View heatmap row"). In `BeliefHeatmap.vue`, add a "→" arrow button on each agent row that opens the journey drawer.
3. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/agents/:agent_name/journey` to the OpenAPI spec under the "Analysis" tag with the full `AgentJourneyResponse` schema. In the simulation article generator, inject journey-aware context when the most-influential agent also has a stance flip: "Agent arc: [name] ([archetype]) began at NO, flipped to YES at round [R] in response to [agent_name]'s post — 'excerpt'. After flipping, [name] influenced [N] other agents." The article uses this to construct a narrative around a specific persuasion moment rather than only describing aggregate trajectory. Add a "View [agent name]'s journey →" link inside the tweet thread export (PR #72) for the simulation's top influencer — making the individual arc shareable as part of the thread.

### 2. Polymarket / Manifold Calibration

**Type:** Integration
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** MiroShark produces consensus numbers: "72% YES." Polymarket and Manifold Markets produce live prediction market prices on the same kinds of questions: "68% YES." Neither platform shows the other. For researchers, analysts, and anyone building on MiroShark's output as a signal, the calibration between simulation consensus and live market prices is the single most credible validation primitive available — more credible than expert quotes, more accessible than academic citation, more concrete than "our agents agreed." A "Calibration" panel on the simulation result page shows: the simulation's final YES/NO/NEUTRAL split, the Polymarket or Manifold market price for a user-specified matching question (fetched at simulation completion), the delta ("MiroShark is 4pp higher than the market"), and a calibration history chart if the same scenario has been run multiple times against the same market. For the "Will a MiroShark simulation be cited in a peer-reviewed paper?" hyperstition, this is the empirical track record that makes citation credible — a simulation that matched Polymarket at ±5pp is a different object than one with no external benchmark. Sandbox note: market URL is pre-fetched before Claude runs using a prefetch script; the result lands in `.xai-cache/market_calibration.json`.
**How:**
1. Add `calibration_market_url: string` (optional) to the simulation launch config (`POST /api/simulation/start` schema and `<sim_dir>/config.json`). The URL must match `polymarket.com/event/` or `manifold.markets/` — validated at launch with a regex check; rejected with HTTP 400 if it matches neither pattern. Create `scripts/prefetch-market-calibration.sh`: for each sim dir with a `calibration_market_url` and no completed calibration cache, fetch the market URL via `curl` (with `--max-time 10` + `--retry 3`), extract the probability from the response HTML (Polymarket uses `og:description` containing "X% chance"; Manifold embeds `probability: 0.NN` in a `<script type="application/json">` tag — a grep on each pattern covers both), and write `{market_url, market_probability_pct: float, fetched_at: ISO, source: "polymarket"|"manifold", market_title: string}` to `.xai-cache/market_calibration/<sim_id>.json`. The workflow runs all `scripts/prefetch-*.sh` before Claude starts, so the cache is always populated before the simulation runner reads it. After simulation completion, the runner reads the cache file (if present) and writes it to `<sim_dir>/calibration.json` alongside the simulation's final `yes_price`, `no_price`, `neutral_price`.
2. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/calibration` returning `{sim_consensus: {yes_pct, no_pct, neutral_pct}, market: {url, title, probability_pct, fetched_at, source}, delta_pp: float, calibration_verdict: "close" (±5pp) | "near" (±10pp) | "divergent" (>10pp), note: string}`. The `note` field is a one-sentence auto-generated description: "MiroShark (72% YES) landed within 4pp of the Polymarket market (68% YES — 'Will ETH break $5K by end of 2026?')." In the simulation launch form (Step 1), add a "Calibration market" optional field below the scenario textarea: a URL input with placeholder `"Paste a Polymarket or Manifold URL…"`. Add validation feedback inline: "✓ Polymarket market detected" or "✓ Manifold market detected" on valid URLs; "Invalid — must be a Polymarket or Manifold market URL" on others. On the simulation result page, when `calibration.json` exists, render a "Calibration" section below the belief drift chart: two side-by-side stat blocks (MiroShark YES% with colored badge, Market YES% with source logo chip), a delta line ("Δ 4pp"), and the calibration verdict chip ("Close — within 5pp"). A "View market →" link opens the market URL in a new tab.
3. In the OpenAPI spec, add `GET /api/simulation/:id/calibration` under the "Analysis" tag with `CalibrationResponse` schema. In `GET /api/simulation/:id` (detail endpoint), add `has_calibration: bool` so the gallery card can show a "📊 Calibrated" chip on simulations with a market comparison. In the public gallery search (`GET /api/gallery`), add a `?calibrated=true` filter that returns only simulations with attached market calibration — making the calibrated cohort a discoverable, high-credibility subset of the gallery. In the article generator, when calibration is present, inject: "Market calibration: simulation consensus (72% YES) vs. [source] market ([market_title]): 68% YES (Δ 4pp — close)." The generated article opens with the calibration figure as a lede: "When MiroShark ran this scenario, its 74-agent swarm reached 72% consensus on YES — within 4 percentage points of what Polymarket traders were pricing at the same time." Document the prefetch pattern in a new `docs/CALIBRATION.md`: how to paste a market URL, how the prefetch runs, and what the `±5pp` threshold means.

### 3. Gallery Community Upvotes

**Type:** Community
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** Published simulations surface in the gallery through two mechanisms: chronological (new first) and trending (surface-stats weighted). Neither mechanism captures direct community quality signal — a simulation with 1 upvote from an expert researcher who read the transcript is worth more than one with 100 watch-page hits from bots, but the current system can't distinguish them. Community Upvotes adds a minimal, portable upvote mechanism: a single thumbs-up button on each gallery card (and on the simulation result page). Votes are stored per-simulation in `<sim_dir>/votes.json` — no auth, no account, one vote per `(ip_hash, sim_id)` pair (SHA-256 of client IP, stored as a counter — not linked back to any identity). The `votes_count` becomes a new sortable field in the gallery, and the leaderboard of most-upvoted simulations of the week becomes a community-curated quality signal entirely distinct from algorithmic trending. For the curator community that builds collections and verified predictions, upvoting is the participation verb that requires no tooling — it's the one-click way to say "this one is worth reading" without needing to write an annotation or build a collection. For new visitors, an "Upvoted" sort on the gallery is a faster quality signal than trending (which rewards virality) or date (which rewards recency).
**How:**
1. Add `POST /api/simulation/:id/upvote` that reads the client IP from `request.remote_addr`, SHA-256-hashes it, and checks `<sim_dir>/votes.json` for a prior vote from this hash. If no prior vote: increment `vote_count`, append `{voter_hash: <sha256_hex>, voted_at: ISO}` to `<sim_dir>/votes.json`, return `{vote_count: N, already_voted: false}`. If prior vote exists: return `{vote_count: N, already_voted: true}` with HTTP 200 (idempotent — no error). Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/votes` returning `{vote_count: int, already_voted: bool}` (checks the current request's IP hash against the stored voter list). The voter hash is never returned to the client or logged outside `votes.json`. In `GET /api/simulation/:id` (detail endpoint), add `vote_count: int` directly to the response so the result page doesn't need a separate request. In `GET /api/gallery` (gallery list endpoint), add `vote_count` to each simulation summary object and add `?sort=votes` as a valid sort option (alongside existing `date`, `trending`, `rounds`, `agents`).
2. In the gallery card component (`SimulationCard.vue`), add a thumbs-up button in the bottom-right of the card: a `👍` icon + vote count. On click: call `POST /api/simulation/:id/upvote`, animate the count incrementing (+1 in place using a CSS scale-pulse animation), and update the button to a filled/active state (persisted in `localStorage` as `upvoted_<sim_id>: true` for the current browser session — client-side dedup complement to the server-side IP hash). On second click (already voted): button shows "✓ Voted" with the count, no re-request. In the gallery toolbar sort dropdown, add "Most upvoted" as a sort option (calls `GET /api/gallery?sort=votes`). On the simulation result page, add an "Upvote this simulation" button below the action chips (alongside "Share," "Embed," "Cite") — same behavior as the gallery card button, larger touch target.
3. Add a "Top upvoted this week" section to the `/explore` gallery page: a horizontal scroll strip above the main grid (same pattern as "Trending" but filtered to `published_at` in the past 7 days and sorted by `vote_count`). The strip shows the top 5 most upvoted simulations of the current week as compact cards. A "See all →" link navigates to `/explore?sort=votes&since=7d`. Add `POST /api/simulation/:id/upvote` and `GET /api/simulation/:id/votes` to the OpenAPI spec under a "Community" tag. Document the IP-hash anonymization in `docs/FEATURES.md`: "Votes are anonymized via SHA-256 hashing of the client IP — MiroShark records whether an IP hash has voted, not which IP it was. No account or login is required." Add `vote_count` to the `surface_stats` response (alongside watch_count, share_count, etc.) so surface analytics tracks upvotes alongside other engagement signals.

### 4. Academic Citation Generator

**Type:** DX / Community
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** MiroShark's `reproduce.json` (PR #75) is a citation primitive for reproducibility — it carries every parameter needed to re-run a simulation. The OriginTrail DKG (PR #84) anchors provenance on-chain. Neither produces a citation a researcher can paste into a paper reference list. Today, a researcher citing a MiroShark simulation has to manually construct a reference: author, title, URL, date, software name, version. The Academic Citation Generator eliminates this by generating properly formatted citations in BibTeX, APA, Chicago, and MLA from a single endpoint. `GET /api/simulation/:id/citation?format=bibtex` returns a formatted BibTeX entry with all required fields auto-populated from simulation metadata: scenario title as the `title` field, the operator's configured instance URL as `howpublished`, `published_at` as the date, "MiroShark" as the `organization` field, and the reproduce.json URL as the `url` field. For academic conferences and journals — the audience that the Jupyter export, per-agent trajectory CSV, quality report, and annotation layer all serve — having a one-click citation is the difference between "I'll manually format this for my bibliography" and "I'll actually include this in the paper." The endpoint is pure string formatting: no LLM, no external calls, 50 lines of Python.
**How:**
1. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/citation?format=bibtex|apa|chicago|mla` (default: `bibtex`). The endpoint reads simulation metadata: `scenario_text` (first 80 chars as title), `published_at`, `n_agents`, `n_rounds`, `model`, and the instance's configured `INSTANCE_URL` (or `http://localhost:5001` fallback). For each format, generate the citation as a plain-text string. **BibTeX**: `@misc{miroshark_<sim_id_first8>, title = {{<scenario_title>}}, author = {{MiroShark}}, year = {<year>}, month = {<month_abbr>}, howpublished = {\url{<instance_url>/share/<sim_id>}}, note = {Simulation: <n_agents> agents, <n_rounds> rounds, model: <model>. Reproducibility config: \url{<instance_url>/api/simulation/<sim_id>/reproduce.json}}}`. **APA**: `MiroShark. (<year>, <Month> <day>). <scenario_title> [Simulation]. <instance_url>/share/<sim_id>`. **Chicago**: `"<scenario_title>." MiroShark, <Month> <day>, <year>. <instance_url>/share/<sim_id>.` **MLA**: `"<scenario_title>." *MiroShark*, <day> <Month> <year>, <instance_url>/share/<sim_id>.` Return the formatted string as `Content-Type: text/plain; charset=utf-8`. Add `?include_reproduce_url=false` parameter to suppress the reproduce.json URL in formats that support inline notes (BibTeX, APA).
2. In the simulation result page's "Cite" section (the existing `EmbedDialog.vue` "Cite" tab), expand the tab to include a format picker: four buttons (BibTeX / APA / Chicago / MLA) rendered as a segmented control. On format selection, fetch `GET /api/simulation/:id/citation?format=<selected>` and display the result in a monospace `<pre>` block with a "Copy" button. The "Copy" button uses the Clipboard API and shows a "✓ Copied!" confirmation for 2 seconds. Persist the user's last-selected format in `localStorage` as `citation_format_preference`. Below the citation block, a one-line note: "Citing as: <format>. The reproduce.json URL is included — it contains all parameters to re-run this simulation identically." In the simulation article generator, inject the BibTeX entry as the first structured data block in the article's YAML front matter (Markdown export): `cite: '@misc{...}'` — making the auto-generated article self-citing out of the box.
3. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/citation` to the OpenAPI spec under a new "Citation" tag with query parameter documentation for `format` and `include_reproduce_url`. Add the endpoint to the Archive Bundle ZIP (PR #92): include `citation.bib` (BibTeX format), `citation-apa.txt`, and `citation-chicago.txt` as three additional files in the archive, alongside the existing manifest entries. The manifest gets three new rows: `{filename: "citation.bib", size, sha256, mime: "text/plain", source_url: "/api/simulation/<id>/citation?format=bibtex"}`. In `docs/FEATURES.md`, add an "Academic Citations" section documenting all four formats with an example citation for each, plus a note: "The BibTeX entry's `note` field includes the reproduce.json URL — reviewers can verify parameters without contacting the operator." Cross-reference the OriginTrail DKG documentation: "For peer-reviewed publications requiring immutable provenance, combine the citation with the on-chain DKG anchor (PR #84) — the DKG UAL uniquely identifies the simulation result at a specific point in time." Add 8 unit tests in `backend/tests/test_unit_citation.py`: each format (4 tests), missing optional fields (scenario_text truncation at 80 chars), `include_reproduce_url=false` (2 tests).

### 5. Simulation Outcome Auto-Resolution

**Type:** Feature / Integration
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** The verified predictions feature (already shipped) requires manual operator annotation: mark "called it," "partial," or "called wrong" after the real-world event happens. Most simulations with future resolution dates never get annotated — not because the outcome wasn't known, but because nobody remembered to go back and mark them. Auto-Resolution closes this loop: at simulation launch, the operator optionally sets a `resolution_date` (ISO date) and a `resolution_url` (a URL whose content will determine the outcome — a news article, a price API, a Polymarket market settlement). On the resolution date, a prefetch script (`scripts/prefetch-resolution-check.sh`) fetches the URL, writes the raw content to `.xai-cache/resolution/<sim_id>.json`, and the simulation runner (next invocation that touches that sim) compares the consensus direction against the content: `resolution_verdict: "verified" | "partial" | "incorrect" | "ambiguous"` with a confidence field based on keyword matching against the simulation's YES/NO semantic pole. Operators can override the auto-verdict from the result page at any time. The auto-resolution signal surfaces in the gallery as a "✓ Auto-verified" badge — distinct from the manually annotated "✓ Verified" badge. For the "Will MiroShark be cited in a peer-reviewed paper?" hyperstition, a track record of auto-verified simulations with documented accuracy rates is the empirical foundation that makes the claim credible.
**How:**
1. Add `resolution_date: ISO date string (optional)` and `resolution_url: string (optional)` to the simulation launch schema (`POST /api/simulation/start`). Validate: `resolution_url` must be an https:// URL if set; `resolution_date` must be in the future if set. Store both in `<sim_dir>/config.json`. Create `scripts/prefetch-resolution-check.sh`: for each sim dir with a `resolution_date` ≤ today and a `resolution_url` and no existing `<sim_dir>/resolution.json`, fetch the `resolution_url` via `curl` (with `--max-time 15 --retry 3`), write `{url, fetched_at: ISO, http_status: int, content: <first 2000 chars of body text, HTML stripped>}` to `.xai-cache/resolution/<sim_id>.json`. In the simulation runner (at the point where `complete.json` is written), if `.xai-cache/resolution/<sim_id>.json` exists for a completed simulation and no `<sim_dir>/resolution.json` exists: load the cached content, check whether the simulation's `final_consensus.direction` (YES/NO/NEUTRAL) aligns with the resolved content. Alignment check: count occurrences of the YES semantic pole words (from the scenario text's top 5 positive-sentiment terms per `ScenarioAnalyzer`, reused if built; otherwise a hardcoded 40-term list) vs. NO pole words in the fetched content. Write `{auto_verdict: "verified"|"partial"|"incorrect"|"ambiguous", auto_confidence: "high"|"medium"|"low", fetched_at: ISO, resolution_url, resolution_date, operator_override: null, final_verdict: <auto_verdict>}` to `<sim_dir>/resolution.json`.
2. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/resolution` returning the `resolution.json` content. Add `POST /api/simulation/:id/resolution/override` accepting `{verdict: "verified"|"partial"|"incorrect"}` — writes `operator_override: verdict` to `resolution.json` and updates `final_verdict`. On the simulation result page, when `resolution_date` is set and today < `resolution_date`: show a "Resolution pending" chip below the scenario title ("Resolves on May 27, 2026") with a countdown ("7 days"). When `resolution.json` exists and `final_verdict` is set: show the verdict chip ("✓ Auto-verified" or "⚠ Auto: Incorrect") alongside a "See resolution source →" link to the resolution URL, and an "Override" button (shows a dropdown: Verified / Partial / Incorrect — fires the override endpoint). In the launch form (Step 1), add two optional fields below the scenario textarea: "Resolution date" (date picker) and "Resolution URL" (text input with "What URL will confirm the outcome?"). Show a note below: "On the resolution date, MiroShark will check this URL and auto-mark the simulation as verified or not."
3. In the gallery, when a simulation has `final_verdict: "verified"`, show a "✓ Auto-verified" green chip on its gallery card (distinct from the manually annotated "✓ Verified" orange chip). Add `?verified=auto` as a new filter on `GET /api/gallery` returning only auto-resolved simulations with `final_verdict: "verified"`. In the `/verified` page (hall of calls), add a tab: "Auto-resolved" showing the auto-verified cohort, sorted by confidence then date. The auto-resolution tab shows an accuracy summary: "N simulations resolved — [X] verified, [Y] incorrect, [Z] partial" as a stat row at the top. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/resolution` to the OpenAPI spec under a "Verification" tag with the `ResolutionResponse` schema. In the archive bundle (PR #92), include `resolution.json` in the ZIP when it exists (manifest entry: `{filename: "resolution.json", ...}`). Document the auto-resolution flow in `docs/FEATURES.md` under "Outcome Verification": explain the prefetch pattern, the alignment check methodology, the confidence tiers, and how to use operator override. Note explicitly: "The auto-verdict is a heuristic — keyword-matching the resolution URL content against the scenario's semantic poles. Treat it as a starting point, not a final judgment. Override when the verdict is clearly wrong."

## Selection Rationale

Five angles distinct from all 30 ideas suggested across May 10, 12, 14, and 16, and from the Scenario Pre-flight Analyzer (May 16 #5, not yet shipped):

- **Agent Journey View** (#1) — The belief heatmap shows stance across all agents per round. The thread view shows all posts in reply structure. Neither tells a single agent's story from their own perspective. The journey view is the character-arc read layer: one agent's stance arc, post history, influence given and received, and flip-moment highlight. Distinct from every prior suggestion because it's agent-centric rather than simulation-centric.
- **Polymarket / Manifold Calibration** (#2) — Simulation consensus ("72% YES") has never been compared to a live prediction market price on the same question. Calibration is the credibility primitive that turns simulation output from "interesting" to "benchmarked." It's the empirical track record the academic-citation hyperstition needs and the quant-tools integration (Trading Signal JSON, PR #91) is missing as a validation layer.
- **Gallery Community Upvotes** (#3) — The trending sort (PR #78) uses surface-stats: watch count, share count, embed count. This rewards distribution, not quality judgment. Upvotes are direct community quality signal — one tap to say "this one is worth reading." The weekly top-upvoted strip is the first editorially curated surface that isn't algorithmic. Distinct from Curator Collections (ordered argument bundles) and Weekly Digest (engagement-weighted auto-curation).
- **Academic Citation Generator** (#4) — `reproduce.json` is machine-readable. OriginTrail DKG is on-chain. Neither is pasteable into a LaTeX bibliography. The citation generator closes the last meter of the academic handoff: one endpoint, four formats, zero friction between "I ran this simulation" and "I cited this simulation." The archive bundle gains three new files (`citation.bib`, `citation-apa.txt`, `citation-chicago.txt`) making every downloaded bundle fully self-contained for paper submission.
- **Simulation Outcome Auto-Resolution** (#5) — The verified predictions feature (already shipped) requires manual annotation. Most future-dated simulations never get annotated because nobody circles back. Auto-resolution stores a resolution URL at launch and checks it automatically on the resolution date via prefetch script — the same pattern as the market calibration prefetch. The "✓ Auto-verified" badge in the gallery is a distinct, time-stamped accuracy signal. Combined with the citation generator (#4) and Polymarket calibration (#2), this makes MiroShark's prediction track record legible and searchable.
