## Ecosystem Context

MiroShark is at 1,099 stars with a 20% fork-to-star ratio that continues to signal active builder engagement, not passive discovery. The distribution stack is now effectively complete: shareable links, spectator watch, tweet threads, OG share cards, trajectory export, transcript export, GIF embeds, RSS feeds. The webhook delivery log (in PR) closes the last observability gap in the automation layer. The platform's surface area is wide enough that new growth comes from depth — statistical rigor, reproducibility, quality signals, and international reach.

The research use case is coming into focus. Issue #70 from Cyril names two problems that MiroShark doesn't yet solve: (1) quantifying simulation output in comparable numeric terms, and (2) modeling private relational dynamics rather than public opinion. The Simulation Impact Scorecard (built) addressed the first. The second remains open. But there's a third dimension Cyril's request implies and didn't state: *reproducibility*. Researchers who want to cite MiroShark results face a legitimacy problem — a single random-seed run is not a scientific finding. It's an illustration.

That reproducibility gap is the thread today's ideas pull on. The five suggestions below address: statistical robustness (Confidence Bands), reproducible intervention protocols (Director Scripts), human-interpretable results (Annotation Layer), run quality control (Quality Guard), and international audience reach (Multilingual Article Export). All five are distinct from the fifteen ideas in the May 2 and May 4 articles and the ten from April 30.

### 1. Multi-Seed Confidence Bands (Ensemble Runs)

**Type:** Feature
**Effort:** Medium (1–2 days)
**Impact:** Every MiroShark simulation today is a single random-seed run. Two runs of the same scenario with different seeds can produce meaningfully different outcomes — not because the scenario is wrong, but because agent sampling is stochastic. A researcher who publishes "73% bearish consensus" from a single run has no statistical claim. Ensemble runs fix this: run the same config 3× with different seeds, compute per-round mean/min/max across runs, and render the belief drift chart as a shaded confidence band (the spread of outcomes) around a mean line. "Consensus emerged in 3 of 3 runs" is a publishable finding. "Consensus in 1 of 3 runs" tells the researcher the scenario is seed-sensitive and they should refine their setup. The feature directly upgrades MiroShark's credibility for the academic and research cohort that represents the most engaged segment of the 219 forks.
**How:**
1. Add `n_runs: int (2–5, default 1)` to the simulation launch config (Step 2). When `n_runs > 1`, the runner executes the simulation sequentially with different random seeds (incrementing a base seed by 1 per run), storing each run in `<ensemble_dir>/run_1/`, `run_2/`, etc. under a parent ensemble directory. The parent sim ID represents the ensemble. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/ensemble` returning `{ensemble_id, n_runs, per_round: [{round, yes_mean, yes_min, yes_max, yes_std}], consensus_rate (fraction of runs reaching consensus), final_yes_mean, final_yes_std}` — aggregated from each run's `trajectory.json`.
2. In the belief drift chart component, detect ensemble simulations (via `ensemble_id` metadata). When present, render two layers: (a) a shaded fill band between `yes_min` and `yes_max` at each round (semi-transparent color, 20% opacity), and (b) a solid `yes_mean` line on top. Add a "Show bands" toggle in the chart toolbar. In the results header, show an ensemble summary chip: "3 runs · consensus in 2/3 · YES: 0.71 ± 0.08."
3. In the article generator, inject the ensemble stats into the prompt context: "Ensemble run: N seeds. Consensus emerged in X of N runs. Final YES price: {mean} ± {std}. Confidence: {high/medium/low} based on std dev threshold (high < 0.10, medium 0.10–0.20, low > 0.20)." The article uses statistically grounded language: "Across 3 independent runs, bearish consensus emerged consistently, reaching 71% (±8%) by round 12. Confidence: high." Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/ensemble` to the OpenAPI spec and document the ensemble export as a CSV with per-round confidence intervals.

### 2. Director Mode Intervention Scripts

**Type:** Feature / DX
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** Director Mode (PR #31) lets users inject narrative events mid-simulation — "breaking news: major exchange delists token" at round 6. It's the most powerful experimental tool in MiroShark and the one most underused, because every intervention is improvised from scratch. A researcher who ran the same intervention sequence across 5 simulations has no reproducible protocol. Intervention Scripts formalize Director Mode into a named, saved, shareable sequence: "at round 3, inject X; at round 7, inject Y." They are the Director Mode equivalent of the template gallery — the transition from one-off exploration to repeatable experimental design. A researcher can now publish: "We applied the Market Shock script (round 3: flash crash −30%; round 7: regulatory clarity) to 6 different scenarios and compared divergence patterns." The shared community script library makes each script a discovery surface for simulation methodology.
**How:**
1. Add an `InterventionScript` resource: `{id, label, description, steps: [{round: int, event: string}], created_at, public: bool}` stored in SQLite. Add `POST /api/intervention-scripts`, `GET /api/intervention-scripts` (list, filterable by `public`), `GET /api/intervention-scripts/:id`. In Step 2 (Configure), add a "Director Script" selector below the completion webhook URL. Users pick from existing scripts or compose a new one inline (add step rows: round number + event text). Scripts are saved to `<sim_dir>/intervention_script.json` at launch.
2. In the simulation runner, at each round start, check if `intervention_script.json` defines a step for the current round number. If so, inject the event string automatically (same code path as the manual Director Mode injection from PR #31). Log each auto-injection to `<sim_dir>/director_log.json` with `{round, event, mode: "script" | "manual"}`. In the results view, add a "Director Events" timeline strip below the belief drift chart: one labeled dot per round that had an injection. Manual and scripted injections styled differently (square vs. circle marker). Tooltip shows full event text.
3. Seed 5 community scripts: **Market Shock** (`[round 3: "flash crash −30%", round 7: "regulatory clarity announced"]`), **Crisis Arc** (`[r2: "whistleblower report leaked", r5: "CEO denies allegations", r9: "regulatory investigation opened"]`), **Recovery Arc** (`[r3: "product recall issued", r6: "independent safety review clears product"]`), **Polarization Engine** (`[r4: "influential contrarian publishes harsh critique", r8: "viral counter-thread emerges"]`), **Catalyst Test** (`[r5: "major institutional buyer announces position"]`). Add a "Publish script" button in the results toolbar (saves the script used in this simulation to the community library). Document all endpoints under a new "Intervention Scripts" tag in the OpenAPI spec.

### 3. Per-Round Annotation Layer

**Type:** DX / Feature
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** A MiroShark simulation result is a belief drift chart and a generated article. Both are machine-produced. The human insight — "this is where the sentiment flipped because the dominant agent changed stance, not because of new information" — lives nowhere in the product today. Per-round annotations let the user attach text notes to specific rounds on the belief drift chart. They appear as vertical dashed markers with labeled badges on the chart, are included in the article and transcript exports, and mark annotated gallery cards with an "Annotated" badge. For researchers sharing simulation results with collaborators or embedding them in papers, annotations are the difference between a black-box chart and a narrated, interpretable record. For gallery discovery, the "Annotated" badge signals that a curator has added context — a proxy quality signal that surfaces the most interpreted simulations first.
**How:**
1. Add `POST /api/simulation/:id/annotations` accepting `{round: int, text: string, visibility: "public" | "private"}`. Store in `<sim_dir>/annotations.json` as a JSON array. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/annotations` and `DELETE /api/simulation/:id/annotations/:annotation_id`. In the belief drift chart component, render annotation markers as vertical dashed lines with a small colored badge at the top (`●R{n}` label). On hover, display the full annotation text in a tooltip. On the spectator watch page (`/watch/<sim_id>`, PR #67), show annotations as they are added in real time.
2. In the simulation results toolbar, add an "Annotate" button. On click, open an inline panel with two fields: round selector (dropdown of round numbers 1–N, with the largest belief-swing round pre-selected: "Biggest swing detected at round 7 — add context?") and a text input (max 280 characters). Save on Enter. Display added annotations as a compact list below the chart. In the article generator, inject visible annotations as structured context before the narrative prompt: "Analyst notes: [Round 3] — regulatory news hit; [Round 7] — dominant agent changed stance without new information." The generated article incorporates these as grounded observations.
3. In the transcript export (Markdown, PR #57), add a "## Simulation Notes" section listing all annotations in round order: `- **Round N** — [text]`. In the public gallery (`/explore`), add an "Annotated" badge (a small notepad icon overlay) on gallery cards where at least one public annotation exists — visible without opening the simulation. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/annotations` to the OpenAPI spec with the full response schema and a note that public annotations are visible without authentication.

### 4. Simulation Quality Guard

**Type:** Feature / DX
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** A dominated simulation — where one agent accounts for 70% of all posts — produces an article that describes the behavior of a single agent, not a swarm. A stagnant simulation — where belief hasn't moved in 5 rounds — is likely converged and will produce no new information in remaining rounds. Both states waste the user's time and compute budget and produce misleading outputs. The Quality Guard runs 4 real-time checks after each round and surfaces actionable warnings on the spectator watch page and results view: narrative dominance, belief stagnation, hard consensus lock, and neutral collapse. Each warning includes a one-sentence diagnosis and a suggested fix ("increase agent count to reduce dominance" or "add a Director Mode event to break stagnation"). For the gallery, clean runs (zero quality flags) earn a "Clean run" badge — a researcher-facing quality signal that distinguishes rigorous simulations from degenerate ones.
**How:**
1. After each round completes, evaluate 4 checks: **(i) Dominance** — if any agent accounts for >50% of this round's posts: flag "Narrative dominated — [agent_name] posted [N]/[M] messages this round. Consider increasing agent count." **(ii) Stagnation** — if |YES price change| across the last 3 rounds < 0.02: flag "Belief stagnant for 3+ rounds — simulation may have converged early. Consider injecting a Director Mode event." **(iii) Hard lock** — if YES price > 0.90 or < 0.10 for 3 consecutive rounds: flag "Hard consensus locked — remaining rounds are unlikely to change outcome." **(iv) Neutral collapse** — if >80% of agents are NEUTRAL with YES < 0.10 and NO < 0.10 for 2 consecutive rounds: flag "Agent consensus collapsed to neutral — scenario may lack sufficient narrative tension." Append all flags to `<sim_dir>/quality_log.json` as `[{round, check, severity: "warning" | "info", message, suggestion}]`.
2. Surface flags as in-UI toasts on the spectator watch page (orange for "warning," grey for "info," auto-dismiss after 8 seconds). In the simulation results view, add a "Quality Report" collapsible section in the sidebar: header shows a green "Clean run" badge if zero warning-severity flags, or an orange "N warnings" count if any. Body lists each flag with round number, message, and suggestion. In the public gallery (`/explore`), add a "Clean" chip filter alongside the existing consensus/quality/sort filters from PR #69 — lets researchers filter to zero-flag simulations only.
3. Add `GET /api/simulation/:id/quality-report` to the OpenAPI spec, returning `{simulation_id, clean: bool, flags: [{round, check, severity, message, suggestion}]}`. In the article generator, inject the quality summary: "Quality: [Clean / N warnings]. [If warnings: Dominance detected round 5; stagnation 3 rounds.] Language adjusted for flagged run." Article language for flagged runs uses hedging: "Results may reflect narrative concentration among a small cluster of agents" rather than "The simulation found that..." Document the four check types and their thresholds in `docs/API.md`.

### 5. Multilingual Article Export

**Type:** Feature
**Effort:** Small (hours)
**Impact:** MiroShark's zh-CN UI toggle (PR #65) proved there is an international audience: the stargazer data today shows 26 new followers from China, Turkey, Indonesia, Colombia, Iran, and Morocco in a single day. The existing internationalization covers the UI only — the simulation narrative article is always generated in English. A French researcher who runs MiroShark gets an English article; a Spanish-speaking startup who uses it for market research publishes English outputs to a Spanish-speaking audience. Adding an `output_language` parameter to the article generator — French, German, Spanish, Japanese, Portuguese, Arabic — costs one prompt instruction and zero new infrastructure. The output is more natural than post-hoc translation because the LLM generates fluent prose in the target language directly from the structured simulation data. For a project where 20% of forks are non-English-speaking communities, this expands the useful output without any UI localization work.
**How:**
1. Add `article_language: string` to the simulation launch config (Step 2 — Configure): a dropdown alongside the existing model preset selector with options `"en" (English — default), "fr" (Français), "de" (Deutsch), "es" (Español), "ja" (日本語), "pt" (Português), "ar" (العربية)`. Store as `article_language` in `<sim_dir>/config.json`. When generating the article, prepend a language instruction to the article generation prompt (before the structured simulation data): `"Generate the simulation narrative report in [language]. Write all section headers, analysis, and commentary in [language]. Agent names and simulation-specific keywords may remain in their original form."` Language-first prompting produces more natural prose than English-then-translate.
2. In the public gallery (`/explore`), add a language filter chip row below the existing filters (from PR #69): `All · EN · ZH · FR · DE · ES · JA · PT · AR`. Store `article_language` as a gallery metadata field at publish time. Gallery cards show a small language badge (e.g., a pill labeled "FR") below the scenario title when not English — visible without opening the simulation. In the RSS/Atom feed (PR #60), include `<dc:language>fr</dc:language>` metadata so feed aggregators (Feedly, n8n, Zapier) can filter subscriptions by language.
3. In the transcript export (Markdown, PR #57), add a header comment when `article_language` is set: `<!-- Article language: [language] — narrative generated in [Language] -->`. In the OpenAPI spec, add `article_language` as an optional string field (enum of supported codes) on the simulation launch schema with an example. In `docs/API.md` and `docs/API.zh-CN.md`, add a "Multilingual Output" section listing supported language codes and a curl example with `"article_language": "fr"`. In the zh-CN UI mode (PR #65), default `article_language` to `"zh"` so Chinese-mode simulations produce Chinese articles by default — closing the current gap where zh-CN UI users still receive English articles.

## Selection Rationale

Five angles distinct from all 25 previously suggested ideas (15 on May 2 + May 4, 10 on April 30) and from the open webhook-delivery-log PR:

- **Multi-Seed Confidence Bands** (#1) — Addresses the reproducibility legitimacy gap directly. A single random-seed run is not a scientific finding. Ensemble runs with confidence bands turn MiroShark into a tool researchers can cite rather than illustrate with. Directly targets the academic and builder cohort that represents the most engaged segment of 219 forks.
- **Director Mode Intervention Scripts** (#2) — Turns the most powerful (and most underused) MiroShark feature into a reproducible research primitive. Director Mode exists since PR #31 — the scripts make it shareable and methodologically rigorous. The community script library is a second discovery surface alongside templates.
- **Per-Round Annotations** (#3) — Human insight has no persistent home in MiroShark today. Annotations give the researcher a voice inside the simulation record, grounding the machine-generated article in human interpretation. The "Annotated" gallery badge creates a curation quality signal that surfaces the most interpreted simulations for discovery.
- **Simulation Quality Guard** (#4) — Prevents wasted runs and misleading outputs from degenerate simulation states. Dominance, stagnation, hard lock, and neutral collapse are all real failure modes that the current product surfaces nowhere. The "Clean run" gallery badge is the flip side: it tells researchers which simulations are methodologically sound before they read them.
- **Multilingual Article Export** (#5) — One prompt instruction, zero new infrastructure, immediate international reach. The stargazer data shows 20%+ of new stars coming from non-English-speaking communities daily. zh-CN UI toggle proved the demand; multilingual article export extends that reach to 6 additional languages for a total of 8 supported output languages.
